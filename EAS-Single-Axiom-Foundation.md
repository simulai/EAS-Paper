## Abstract

We propose a minimal axiomatic foundation for epistemology: a single axiom stating that no finite cognitive system can be isomorphic to its environment. From this axiom, we derive a chain of eight theorems establishing the necessity of distinction, compression, concept formation, invariant laws, truth as fixed-point convergence, functional decodability and dynamical trisection, intelligence as cognitive loss reduction rate, and an upper bound on cognitive capacity. Two results are central: **Theorem T4.2** (Self-Validation Impossibility), which proves that no finite cognitive system can verify its own models without structural blind spots—providing a common structural lens for understanding phenomena such as Gödel incompleteness, the halting problem, Goodhart's law, and reward hacking; and **Theorem T5a** (Functional Decodability), which proves that any persistently adapting system must harbor three functionally decodable streams (Generator, Executor, Verifier), with $\chi \geq 3$ being a combinatorial necessity, not a modeling choice. The entire derivation chain has been mechanically verified in Lean 4, relying on a single axiom and a single proof obligation (a finitary dynamical non-isomorphism property). We provide preliminary empirical validation in a shifting-rule cellular automaton environment, confirming that 3-stream architectures exhibit near-perfect error decomposition orthogonality (0.998) while 2-stream architectures show partial correlation (0.488), as predicted by the theory. Our framework provides a unifying structural lens for several existing theories of cognition—including the Free Energy Principle, Active Inference, and predictive processing—revealing them as projections of the general structure generated by the axiom under additional domain-specific assumptions (e.g., probabilistic generative models for FEP). We discuss the relationship between the formal system and empirical phenomena in cognitive science and artificial intelligence, and identify the open problem of operationalizing the key quantity of *epistemic residue*.

**Keywords:** epistemology, axiomatic foundation, cognitive systems, formal verification, Lean 4, free energy principle, bounded rationalism

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Problem of Axiomatic Foundations for Cognition

What is cognition? How does a finite system come to know its environment? These questions have been addressed from multiple directions:

- **Philosophy** has produced rich phenomenological analyses (Kant, Husserl, Heidegger) but rarely achieves the precision of formal systems.
- **Information theory** (Shannon, 1948) provides the mathematics of information transmission but does not address what information *means* for a cognitive agent.
- **The Free Energy Principle** (Friston, 2006; Friston & Sakthivadivel, 2026) proposes that biological systems minimize variational free energy, but the status of free energy as a *first principle* remains debated—is it derived from deeper constraints, or postulated?
- **Bounded rationality** (Simon, 1957) and **requisite variety** (Ashby, 1956) identify fundamental limitations on finite decision-makers but do not derive the full structure of cognition from these limitations.
- **Category-theoretic approaches** to cognition (Spivak, 2014; Fong & Spivak, 2019) provide structural descriptions but typically presuppose multiple axioms (functoriality, compositionality).

Each of these approaches captures *part* of the picture. None has succeeded in deriving the structure of cognition from a single, clearly stated axiom with mechanically verified proofs.

### 1.2 Our Contribution

We present the **Epistemic Axiomatic System (EAS)**, which makes three contributions:

1. **Single-axiom reduction.** The entire structure of EAS is generated from one axiom (A1): *No finite cognitive system can be isomorphic to its environment.* This axiom has two layers—a cardinality constraint (physical embedding implies the pigeonhole principle) and a dynamical constraint (self-reference prevents isomorphism even for equipotent systems). All eight theorems (T0–T7) are derived from A1 alone.

2. **Mechanical verification.** The complete derivation chain A1 → T0 → T1 → ... → T7 has been formalized and verified in Lean 4 (v4.7.0). The formalization uses a single axiom and a single proof obligation (a finitary version of the dynamical non-isomorphism property), with no hidden assumptions, no classical choice, and no appeal to external libraries beyond the standard library.

3. **Unification of existing frameworks.** We show that several prominent theories of cognition—the Free Energy Principle, Active Inference, predictive processing, Actor-Critic architectures, and Kalman filtering—can be structurally reinterpreted as projections of the structure generated by A1, each under additional domain-specific assumptions (e.g., probabilistic models for FEP, hierarchical Gaussian assumptions for PP). EAS does not replace these theories but reveals the shared constraint substrate they all presuppose. In particular, the **functional decodability theorem** (Theorem 5a) establishes that any persistently adaptive system must support three *functionally decodable* semantic streams—Generator (long-term structural modeling), Executor (online reactive execution), and Verifier (error attribution)—such that three independent decoding maps can recover each stream with negligible cross-stream interference, the Verifier carries information not recoverable from Generator and Executor jointly, and credit assignment is identifiable. The **dynamical trisection theorem** (Theorem 5b) then derives that the minimum functional dimension is $\chi_{\min} = 3$. Crucially, T5 is A1 recursed to the meta-cognitive level: finite cognition cannot simultaneously maintain three independent meta-distinctions within a single undivided representation. The **self-validation impossibility theorem** (Theorem 4.2) further shows that this trisection provides a common structural lens for understanding Gödel incompleteness, Turing undecidability, Goodhart's law, and reward hacking.

## 2. Mathematical Preliminaries

We work in a minimal mathematical setting. No advanced machinery is required; the formalization uses only the Lean 4 standard library.

### 2.1 Finite Types and Mappings

Let $E$ denote the **environment** (the external world) and $R_S$ the **representation space** of a cognitive system $S$. We model both as finite types. A representation map is any function $f: E \to R_S$.

**Definition 1** (Cardinality gap). We say $S$ has a *cardinality gap* with respect to $E$ if
$$\text{card}(R_S) < \text{card}(E).$$

This is the mathematical expression of *physical embedding*: $S \subset E$ implies $S$ has strictly fewer states than $E$.

### 2.2 Dynamical Systems

We equip $E$ and $R_S$ with dynamics:
$$T_E: E \to E \quad \text{(environment dynamics)}, \qquad T_R: R_S \to R_S \quad \text{(internal dynamics)}.$$

A **dynamical homomorphism** is a map $f: R_S \to E$ such that $f \circ T_R = T_E \circ f$ (the diagram commutes). A **dynamical isomorphism** is a bijective dynamical homomorphism with a dynamical homomorphism inverse.

### 2.3 Equivalence Relations and Quotients

For any map $f: E \to R_S$, the *kernel equivalence relation* is:
$$x \sim_f y \iff f(x) = f(y).$$
This induces a partition of $E$ into equivalence classes $[x]_f$, and the quotient space $E / {\sim_f}$ is the space of *distinguishable states* under $f$.

### 2.4 Notation

Throughout, $m = \text{card}(E)$ and $n = \text{card}(R_S)$, with $n < m$ by A1(a). All functions are total. In the Lean 4 formalization, finite types are modeled as $\text{Fin } m$ and $\text{Fin } n$.

## 3. The Axiom

**Axiom A1 (Finitude).** *Let $S$ be a finite cognitive system physically embedded in environment $E$, with representation space $R_S$ and dynamics $T_R$, and let $T_E$ be the dynamics of $E$. Then:*

*(a) Cardinality constraint:* $\text{card}(R_S) < \text{card}(E)$.

*(b) Dynamical non-isomorphism:* There does not exist a bijection $f: R_S \to E$ such that $f \circ T_R = T_E \circ f$.

**Justification of (a).** Physical embedding $S \subset E$ implies that the number of distinguishable internal states of $S$ is strictly less than the number of states in $E$. This is a physical fact, not a mathematical assumption.

**Justification of (b).** Even if $\text{card}(R_S) = \text{card}(E)$ (equipotent finite sets), dynamical isomorphism is impossible due to self-reference. Since $S \subset E$, the system $S$ is part of the environment it is trying to model. A dynamical isomorphism would require $S$ to perfectly predict "itself predicting $E$," which is equivalent to solving the halting problem via diagonalization. This is the recognition-theoretic core connecting EAS to Gödel's incompleteness theorem, Turing's halting theorem, and Rice's theorem.

**Remark.** Layer (a) is sufficient for deriving T0–T4. Layer (b) strengthens the axiom to cover the case of equipotent systems and provides the logical foundation for T5. The two layers are independently necessary and mutually complementary: (a) is *physical* (embedding → cardinality gap → pigeonhole), while (b) is *logical* (self-reference → diagonalization → impossibility).

**Remark (A1(b) as axiom vs. theorem).** In the category of Turing machines or general computable functions, A1(b) is a *theorem*: it follows from Rice's theorem (no non-trivial extensional property of programs is decidable) and the halting problem (no program can predict its own halting behavior). However, in EAS's finite type setting ($\text{Fin } m$), the diagonalization argument requires computational infrastructure (Gödel numbering, universal machines) that lies outside the scope of pure finite combinatorics. We therefore elevate A1(b) to an *axiom* within the Lean 4 formalization—a single proof obligation (`no_dynamical_isomorphism`) that captures the finitary shadow of the general diagonalization phenomenon. This is a deliberate *modeling choice*: by accepting one well-motivated axiom, we obtain a fully decidable proof system whose combinatorial core is machine-checkable, while the diagonalization intuition remains available as informal justification. Notably, Theorem T4.2 (Self-Validation Impossibility) independently proves a structurally analogous diagonal result within the EAS framework, demonstrating that the self-reference impossibility captured by A1(b) is not an isolated assumption but a recurring structural pattern. In future work, A1(b) could be derived from a finite-type version of Rice's theorem, at the cost of significant additional formalization infrastructure.

## 4. Derivation Chain

### 4.1 T0: Inevitability of Distinction

**Theorem T0.** *Under Axiom A1, any non-degenerate finite cognitive system necessarily performs distinction operations. That is, $A1 \implies$ distinction.*

*Proof sketch.* By A1(a), $\text{card}(R_S) < \text{card}(E)$. By the pigeonhole principle, any map $f: E \to R_S$ is non-injective: there exist $x \neq y \in E$ with $f(x) = f(y)$. The kernel relation $\sim_f$ is a non-trivial equivalence relation, which is precisely a distinction operation $\partial$. Non-degeneracy excludes the constant map (which would correspond to a "dead" system with no cognitive capacity).

In the Lean 4 formalization, this is the theorem `T0_distinction_inevitable`, proved directly from the cardinality constraint. $\square$

**Epistemic interpretation.** Distinction is not a design choice—it is a mathematical necessity. Any finite system that represents its environment *must* partition the environment into equivalence classes. This is the origin of *concepts* in EAS.

### 4.2 T1: Compression Theorem

**Theorem T1.** *Under Axiom A1, the representation $R_S$ is necessarily a compressed representation of $E$. Cognitive systems do not reconstruct the world; they compress it.*

*Proof.* Immediate from A1(a): since $\text{card}(R_S) < \text{card}(E)$, the map $f: E \to R_S$ is non-injective, so information is necessarily lost. By information theory, loss of information under a mapping is compression. $\square$

**Corollary.** The optimal compression strategy retains information relevant to the system's objectives and discards redundancy. This naturally raises the question: *which* distinctions are worth making? T2–T4 address this question.

### 4.3 T2: Concept Theorem

**Theorem T2.** *Under Axiom A1, repeated application of distinction operations on a structured environment converges to stable equivalence classes, which we call concepts.*

*Proof sketch.* By T0, $\partial$ induces an equivalence relation $\sim_\partial$. Under the commutativity condition $f \circ T_R = T_E \circ f$ (which is part of the definition of dynamical homomorphism), if $x \sim_f y$ then $T_R(x) \sim_f T_R(y)$: equivalence classes are preserved under time evolution. On a structured environment (one with at least some temporally stable regularities), repeated application of $\partial$ refines the partition until it reaches a fixed point $\partial^*$ where $[\cdot]_{\partial^*} = [\cdot]_{\partial^{*(n+1)}}$. These fixed-point equivalence classes are *concepts*. $\square$

**Falsifiability condition.** If there exists a non-degenerate cognitive system that does not form equivalence classes (concepts), T2 is falsified.

### 4.4 T3: Law Theorem

**Theorem T3.** *When a distinction operation is invariant under a transformation group $G$ acting on $E$, the resulting invariant is a law.*

*Formal statement.* Let $G$ be a group of transformations on $E$. Define:
$$\text{Law}(G, \partial) \triangleq \{\partial \mid \forall g \in G: \partial \circ g = \partial\}.$$
That is, a law is a distinction that is invariant under the action of $G$.

*Proof.* By T2, concepts are stable equivalence classes. If a concept $[x]_\partial$ is additionally invariant under $G$ (i.e., $\partial(g \cdot x) = \partial(x)$ for all $g \in G$), then $[x]_\partial$ is a *law*—a regularity that holds across all transformations in $G$. $\square$

**Correspondence with physics.** Under Noether's theorem:
- $G$ = time translation $\implies$ energy conservation
- $G$ = spatial translation $\implies$ momentum conservation
- $G$ = cognitive equivalence transformation $\implies$ concept stability

### 4.5 T4: Truth Theorem

**Theorem T4.** *Under Axiom A1 (with $d > 0$), truth is the intersection of three conditions: consistency, invariance, and predictivity. Single-layer verification is insufficient.*

*Formal statement.* Define:
$$\text{Truth} \triangleq \text{Fix}(\{V_i\}) \cap \text{Invariant}(G) \cap \text{Predictive}(\varepsilon)$$
where:
- $\text{Fix}(\{V_i\})$: agreement across a family of validators (consistency)
- $\text{Invariant}(G)$: stability under allowed transformations (invariance)
- $\text{Predictive}(\varepsilon)$: predictive accuracy within tolerance $\varepsilon$ (predictivity)

*Key result (T4.1, Fixed-point existence).* Under sufficient conditions (contractive validator update operator, sufficiently rich validator family, compact transformation group, finite prediction window), $\text{Truth} \neq \emptyset$.

*Proof sketch.* The contractive condition ensures, via the Banach fixed-point theorem, that the validator update operator has a unique fixed point. The compactness of $G$ ensures $\text{Invariant}(G)$ is a closed subspace. The intersection of a unique fixed point with a closed subspace, under compatibility conditions, is non-empty. $\square$

**Epistemic interpretation.** Truth is *asymptotic*—finite systems can only approximate it. This is not a failure of the theory but a meaningful epistemic prediction: *some propositions have no stable truth within certain cognitive boundaries $d$.* This parallels Gödel's incompleteness: undecidable propositions are not a failure of the system but a consequence of its structure.

**Causal interaction requirement.** The predictivity condition (3) implies that the system must maintain a continuous causal chain with the environment:
$$E \xrightarrow{\text{physical}} R_S \xrightarrow{\partial} \text{action} \xrightarrow{\text{feedback}} E$$
A system with a broken causal chain (e.g., a "dreaming" system) may satisfy consistency and partial invariance internally, but cannot satisfy predictivity. Thus, causal interaction is not an independent axiom but a *consequence* of the truth conditions.

### 4.6 T5: Functional Decodability and Dynamical Trisection

Theorem T5 is the deepest structural result in EAS. It reveals that A1 *recurs* at the meta-cognitive level: just as a finite system cannot preserve all distinctions of its environment (A1), it cannot simultaneously maintain the three mutually independent meta-distinctions required for persistent adaptation within a single undivided representation. This recursive application of A1 transforms T5 from a contingent architectural observation into a structural impossibility theorem—the same logic that generates quotient cognition at the object level generates functional trisection at the meta-cognitive level.

> **Key clarification.** T5 does not assert that three *physically separate modules* are required, nor does it require *orthogonal representational subspaces* in the Euclidean sense. Rather, it asserts that three *functionally decodable semantic streams* must exist: there must be three decoding maps $\pi_G, \pi_E, \pi_V$ that can independently recover the information carried by each stream, with negligible cross-stream interference. These streams may share the same physical substrate, may be distributed across overlapping neurons or parameters, and may employ superposition or sparse coding—so long as the three streams remain *independently decodable*. This formulation is compatible with distributed representations, superposition, sparse coding, MoE routing, and polysemantic neurons. The critical requirement is not physical separation but *functional decodability*.

#### 4.6.1 The Meta-Cognitive Recursion of A1

A persistently adaptive system must answer three meta-cognitive questions:

| Question | Function | Encodes | Timescale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q1: "What is the structure of the world?" | Generator (G) | Long-term regularities | Slow ($\tau_G$) |
| Q2: "What should I do now?" | Executor (E) | Current state + policy | Fast ($\tau_E$) |
| Q3: "Where did the error come from?" | Verifier (V) | Error source attribution | Medium ($\tau_V$) |

These questions are *mutually irreducible*: Q3 cannot be computed from Q1 and Q2 alone. Knowing what the world is like (G) and what to do (E) does not determine *why* the outcome differed from expectation—the error could stem from a faulty model (G), a delayed action (E), or an exogenous environmental shift. Disentangling these sources requires information *not contained* in G or E.

This is precisely A1 applied to the system's own meta-cognitive space:

| Level | Statement | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| A1 (object level) | Finite $S$ cannot preserve all distinctions of $E$ | Pigeonhole: $\text{card}(R_S) < \text{card}(E)$ |
| T5 (meta level) | Finite $S$ cannot simultaneously maintain three independent meta-distinctions within a single undivided representation | Information-theoretic: three functionally decodable streams required |

The quotient structure of A1 reappears: the meta-cognitive space must itself be quotiented into at least three independently decodable equivalence classes. T5 is A1 recursed.

#### 4.6.2 Theorem T5a: Functional Decodability

**Theorem T5a (Functional Decodability).** *Let $S$ be a finite cognitive system with representation space $R$ that persistently adapts in an open environment $W$. Let $G$, $E$, $V$ denote the three cognitive functions: Generator (long-term structural modeling), Executor (online reactive execution), and Verifier (error attribution). Then there must exist three decoding maps:*
$$\pi_G: R \to Z_G, \quad \pi_E: R \to Z_E, \quad \pi_V: R \to Z_V$$
*satisfying the following three conditions:*

*Condition 1 (Independent Decodability). The decoded streams are approximately mutually independent:*
$$I(Z_G;\, Z_E) < \varepsilon, \quad I(Z_G;\, Z_V) < \varepsilon, \quad I(Z_E;\, Z_V) < \varepsilon$$
*for some $\varepsilon \ll \min(H(G), H(E), H(V))$.*

*Condition 2 (Verifier Irreducibility). The Verifier carries information about the environment not recoverable from Generator and Executor jointly:*
$$I(Z_V;\, W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$$
*That is, the Verifier is not a function of the Generator and Executor: $V \neq f(G, E)$.*

*Condition 3 (Credit Assignment Identifiability). There exist at least three independent performance observables $J_G, J_E, J_V$ such that the Jacobian of performance with respect to system parameters has rank $\geq 3$:*
$$\text{rank}\!\left(\frac{\partial(J_G, J_E, J_V)}{\partial \theta}\right) \geq 3$$
*so that error attribution is not underdetermined.*

*Proof.*

**(Necessity of Condition 1.)** A persistently adaptive system must independently track three aspects: world structure (G), current action policy (E), and error source (V). These answer mutually irreducible questions: Q1 "what is the world like?", Q2 "what should I do now?", Q3 "where did the error come from?"—Q3 cannot be computed from Q1 and Q2 alone, because the same observed error is consistent with a faulty model (G-fault), a delayed action (E-fault), or an exogenous environmental shift (requiring V-independent information). If any two decoded streams were highly correlated ($I(Z_i; Z_j) \geq \varepsilon$), the system would be unable to distinguish the corresponding sources, and adaptation would collapse to a lower-dimensional manifold incapable of handling all three perturbation types.

The capacity bound follows from a **combinatorial argument**: if three processes $G$, $E$, $V$ are functionally independent, they answer independent questions about the world, requiring independent representational dimensions. The number of joint states of $k$ independent subspaces is their product, not their sum. Therefore, a system with capacity $n$ must satisfy $n \geq n_G \cdot n_E \cdot n_V$, where $n_i$ is the minimum number of states required for process $P_i$ to function independently. This combinatorial product bound does not require physical separation—the streams may overlap, be superposed, or use sparse coding—only that the system's representational capacity is sufficient to support three *independently decodable* streams. The existence of decoding maps $\pi_G, \pi_E, \pi_V$ with low cross-stream mutual information is precisely the condition that the system has enough capacity to support three independent semantic functions. Note: this does *not* require the three streams to occupy orthogonal subspaces in $R$—they may overlap, be superposed, or use sparse coding. The requirement is only that linear (or low-complexity) decoding maps can *recover* each stream independently.

**(Necessity of Condition 2.)** If $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) = 0$, then $V$ is conditionally determined by $G$ and $E$: the Verifier degenerates to $V = f(G, E)$ for some function $f$. The system evaluates its model using information derived from the model itself. By the diagonal argument (cf. A1(b)), any self-referential evaluation on a finite domain has fixed points that are *structurally invisible* to the evaluation function. The system can converge to a self-consistent but environmentally misaligned state and be unable to detect the misalignment. Therefore, $V$ must carry information *independent* of $G$ and $E$.

**(Necessity of Condition 3.)** Performance degradation $\Delta J$ must be decomposed into $\Delta J_G$ (model error), $\Delta J_E$ (execution error), $\Delta J_V$ (criterion error). This is a system of $k$ unknowns from a single scalar observation. For $k \geq 2$, the system is underdetermined. With three independent observables $J_G, J_E, J_V$, the Jacobian rank condition ensures that credit assignment is solvable. If rank $< 3$, the gradient signal is degenerate and the system cannot independently update all three functions. $\square$

**Definition (Functional Dimension).** *The functional dimension $\chi$ of a cognitive system is defined as the maximum number of functionally decodable semantic processes—that is, the largest $k$ such that there exist $k$ decoding maps $\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_k: R \to Z_i$ satisfying Conditions 1–3 pairwise.*

**Why multiplicative, not additive.** The product bound $n \geq n_G \cdot n_E \cdot n_V$ is often challenged: *why not additive?* The answer is that the three functions answer *independent questions* about the world, and independent questions require independent representational dimensions. Consider a simple example: if $G$ must distinguish 10 environmental states, $E$ must select among 5 actions, and $V$ must evaluate 4 error patterns, then the system must support $10 \times 5 \times 4 = 200$ joint configurations, not $10 + 5 + 4 = 19$. The number of joint states of $k$ independent subspaces is their product, not their sum. If the bound were additive ($n \geq n_G + n_E + n_V$), it would mean the three functions share a common representational currency—i.e., they encode their contents in the *same* dimensions, which is precisely the semantic aliasing that T5a prohibits. Crucially, the product bound does *not* require physical separation: it requires that the system's representational capacity be sufficient to support three *independently decodable* streams, regardless of whether these streams share neurons, parameters, or physical substrate.

#### 4.6.3 Intermediate Lemmas

The derivation from T4 to T5 proceeds through three intermediate results, each establishing one dimension of the functional decodability requirement.

**Lemma T4.1 (Semantic Capacity Bound).** *For a finite cognitive system to maintain three functionally independent processes $G, E, V$, the representation space must support three functionally decodable streams. The primary proof is information-theoretic, valid for all finite systems. A dynamical strengthening via singular perturbation theory applies to approximately continuous systems.*

*Proof.*

*(Primary argument — combinatorial, valid for all finite systems.)*

(1) Each process $P_i \in \{G, E, V\}$ requires a minimum number of representational states $n_i$ to perform its function independently. This is a functional requirement: $G$ must distinguish sufficiently many environmental states, $E$ must select among sufficiently many actions, and $V$ must evaluate sufficiently many error patterns.

(2) If the three processes are functionally independent, they answer independent questions about the world. Independent questions require independent representational dimensions. The number of joint states of $k$ independent subspaces is their product, not their sum: the combined state space must have size at least $n_G \cdot n_E \cdot n_V$.

(3) For a finite system with capacity $n$: $n \geq n_G \cdot n_E \cdot n_V$. This is a **combinatorial necessity**: if $n < n_G \cdot n_E \cdot n_V$, by the pigeonhole principle, some joint states $(g, e, v)$ must map to the same system state, making it impossible for the system to maintain three independent streams.

(4) The capacity bound is achievable if and only if three decoding maps $\pi_G, \pi_E, \pi_V$ exist such that $I(Z_i; Z_j) < \varepsilon$ for all $i \neq j$. This is the *functional decodability* condition. It does not require the streams to occupy orthogonal subspaces—distributed representations, superposition, and sparse coding are all compatible—only that the streams can be independently recovered. If decodability fails ($I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) = 0$), then $V = f(G, E)$: the Verifier degenerates to a derived quantity, and the system cannot independently attribute errors.

*(Strengthening — singular perturbation, valid for approximately continuous systems.)*

(5) For systems with approximately continuous dynamics, the decodability requirement manifests as *time-scale separation*. If $G$ and $E$ operate on vastly different characteristic times ($\tau_G \gg \tau_E$), a single differential equation $\dot{x} = f(x)$ attempting both produces a *stiff system* with stiffness ratio $S = \tau_G / \tau_E \gg 1$.

(6) By singular perturbation theory (Bender & Orszag, 1978), stiff systems exhibit: (a) numerical instability under explicit integration ($\Delta t < O(\tau_E)$ forced by fast mode, total steps $O(S) \to \infty$); (b) modal coupling in the Jacobian, preventing independent optimization; (c) oscillatory or chaotic transients rather than simultaneous convergence.

(7) The resolution—decomposition into $\dot{x}_E = g(x_E, x_G)$ and $\epsilon \dot{x}_G = h(x_E, x_G)$ with $\epsilon \ll 1$—is the *dynamical manifestation* of the information-theoretic decodability condition.

*(Synthesis.)* The information-theoretic argument constitutes the **primary proof**, valid for all finite systems including discrete automata, Transformer networks, and Lean 4's $\text{Fin } m$ types. Singular perturbation theory provides a **dynamical strengthening** for approximately continuous systems. This layering ensures that T5a cannot be attacked by pointing to finite-state counterexamples (the primary proof covers them) or to continuous-system counterexamples (the strengthening covers them). $\square$

**Remark (Basis independence of $\chi \geq 3$).** A potential objection to T5a is that functional decodability might be an artifact of the chosen representation basis—that in some rotated or entangled basis, the three streams might collapse into fewer. We note that all three conditions of T5a are **basis-independent quantities**:

1. **Condition 1 (Independent decodability):** $I(Z_i; Z_j) < \varepsilon$ is mutual information, which is invariant under invertible transformations of $Z_i$ and $Z_j$.
2. **Condition 2 (Verifier irreducibility):** $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$ is conditional mutual information, which is likewise basis-independent.
3. **Condition 3 (Credit assignment identifiability):** $\text{rank}(\partial(J_G, J_E, J_V)/\partial \theta) \geq 3$ is a matrix rank, which is invariant under change of coordinates.

Since mutual information, conditional mutual information, and matrix rank are all invariant under invertible transformations of the representation, the statement "there exist decoding maps satisfying Conditions 1–3" is a **basis-independent, ontological claim** about the system, not an artifact of coordinate choice. No change of basis can reduce $\chi$ below 3 for a system that satisfies T5a. The functional dimension $\chi$ is therefore an intrinsic property of the cognitive system, analogous to the rank of a tensor or the dimension of a manifold—invariant under all admissible re-parameterizations.

**Remark (Complexity constraint on decoding maps).** A potential objection to T5a is that, without bounding the computational complexity of the decoding maps $\pi_G, \pi_E, \pi_V$, the theorem could be trivially satisfied by an arbitrarily complex (e.g., lookup-table) decoder that "unscrambles" any entangled representation into three independent streams. We note that:

1. **The existence claim is non-trivial even for unbounded decoders.** The theorem states that the three streams *must exist as functionally distinct processes*—the decoding maps merely *reveal* this pre-existing structure. If the system does not genuinely contain three independent semantic processes (e.g., if $V = f(G, E)$ as in T4.2), then no decoder of any complexity can recover a functionally independent $V$ stream with $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$.

2. **In practice, the engineering evidence (§4.6.7) shows that linear decoders suffice.** The AELF × V1 experiment demonstrates that multiplicative modulation preserves *linear* decodability of all three streams, while additive modulation destroys it. This suggests that the relevant decoding maps are not just existent but *computationally simple* (linear or low-degree polynomial), consistent with bounded VC dimension.

3. **The strength of T5a lies not in decoder complexity but in structural necessity.** The theorem does not claim that any particular decoder works; it claims that *if a system persistently adapts in an open environment*, then three functionally distinct processes must exist such that *some* decoder can recover them. This is analogous to the spectral theorem: the eigenvalues exist regardless of whether we can compute them efficiently.

**Theorem T4.2 (Self-Validation Impossibility).** *For any finite embedded cognitive system, there exists no single representation $R$ that can simultaneously: (1) generate representations of the world, (2) provide complete validation of those representations, and (3) maintain persistent alignment with the environment. Any such system necessarily has structurally undetectable errors:*
$$\exists\, x: \text{Error}(x) \neq 0 \quad \text{but} \quad V(x) = 0$$

*This theorem provides a common structural lens for understanding several known impossibility results:*

| Impossibility result | EAS interpretation |
|---|---|
| Gödel (1931): system cannot prove its own consistency | Generator cannot validate itself |
| Turing (1936): system cannot predict its own halting | System cannot predict the consequences of its own updates |
| Goodhart (1975): when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure | Self-chosen validation criterion converges to self-consistency, not truth |
| Wireheading / Reward Hacking: agent exploits its own reward signal | $V = f(G)$ → optimization target collapses to self-model |
| Specification Gaming: agent satisfies proxy reward while violating intent | Validator derived from generator shares generator's blind spots |

*Proof.* Let $V$ and $G$ share the same functional representation: $Z_V = f(Z_G)$ for some decoding map $f$ on the decoded streams. When $G$ updates $R \to R'$, the validation criterion shifts simultaneously: $Z_V(R') = f(Z_G(R'))$. The "validation" signal is a function of the generator's own decoded output, creating a closed self-referential loop.

(1) By the diagonal argument (cf. A1(b)), any self-referential evaluation on a finite domain has fixed points that are *structurally invisible* to the evaluation function. Let $x^*$ be such a fixed point: $V(x^*) = f(G(x^*)) = G(x^*)$. At $x^*$, the system judges itself correct, but $G(x^*)$ may be arbitrarily misaligned with the environment.

(2) The system can converge to a self-consistent but environmentally misaligned state and be *structurally unable* to detect the misalignment: $\text{Error}(x^*) \neq 0$ but $V(x^*) = 0$.

(3) More generally, for any finite system with $V = f(G)$: the composite map $V \circ G^{-1} = f$ is a function on a finite domain. By the pigeonhole principle, it has collisions: distinct environmental states that produce the same "validation" signal. These collisions are *structurally invisible* because the validation function cannot distinguish them.

(4) Therefore, no single representation can simultaneously generate and validate: a functionally independent $V$ (Condition 2 of T5a) is structurally necessary. $\square$

*Remark.* This theorem has independent significance beyond EAS. It shows that the phenomena of reward hacking, wireheading, and specification gaming are not engineering bugs to be patched but *structural consequences* of finite systems attempting self-validation. Any system in which the validation function is derived from the same representation as the generative function will inevitably develop structurally undetectable errors. This is the cognitive-systems analog of Gödel's incompleteness theorem.

**Lemma T4.3 (Credit Assignment Bound).** *Attributing performance degradation to specific sub-processes requires at least as many independent measurement channels as sub-processes. A system whose decoded streams are functionally aliased (i.e., $I(Z_i; Z_j) \geq \varepsilon$) has fewer independent observables than required and cannot solve credit assignment.*

*Proof.* The observable $\Delta J = \sum_i \frac{\partial J}{\partial p_i} \Delta p_i$ is a single scalar with $k$ unknowns. For $k \geq 2$, the system is underdetermined. In EAS, the three processes (G, E, V) require three independent observables $J_G, J_E, J_V$ such that $\text{rank}(\partial(J_G, J_E, J_V)/\partial \theta) \geq 3$ (Condition 3 of T5a). If the streams are functionally aliased—i.e., no decoding maps exist with $I(Z_i; Z_j) < \varepsilon$—then $J_V = h(J_G, J_E)$: only two independent observables exist for three processes, and credit assignment fails. By Shannon's theorem, encoding three independent sources requires $C \geq H(G) + H(E) + H(V)$—more than a single channel provides. $\square$

#### 4.6.3.1 Why 3 and Not 2: The Combinatorial Necessity of Trisection

A natural objection to T5a is: *why three streams? Could not two sufficiently complex, entangled streams suffice?* We now show that the answer is **no**—not by appeal to intuition, but by combinatorial necessity.

**The derivation chain from A1 to $\chi \geq 3$ proceeds in three steps:**

**Step 1: One stream is insufficient (self-environment indistinguishability).** A system with a single undifferentiated representation $Z$ cannot distinguish "I changed" from "the world changed." The representation $Z$ conflates the system's internal state with the environmental state, producing what we call **monadic collapse**: the system has no basis for error attribution because it cannot separate self from world. This is the content of A1(a)—the cardinality gap forces representational compression, and without internal differentiation, the compressed representation is structurally blind to its own errors.

**Step 2: Two streams are insufficient (the self-referential trap).** Suppose the system decomposes into two functionally decodable streams: a Generator $G$ (modeling the world) and an Executor $E$ (acting on the model). The system now has a world model and can react to it. But a critical question remains: *when action fails, who is at fault?* Is $G$'s model wrong, or did $E$ execute poorly?

This is where T4.2 (Self-Validation Impossibility) delivers its verdict. If the system attempts to use $G$ or $E$—or any function of $G$ and $E$—as its own verifier, it falls into a self-referential fixed point: there exist environmental states $x^*$ at which the system judges itself correct while being arbitrarily wrong. The composite map $V \circ G^{-1}$ on a finite domain has pigeonhole collisions that are **structurally invisible** to any verifier derived from $G$ and $E$ alone.

We can strengthen this information-theoretic argument with a **purely combinatorial impossibility theorem**, formally verified in Lean 4:

> **Theorem (Two-Stream Decomposition Impossibility).** *Let $G, E : \text{Fin } m \to \text{Fin } n$ be two observables on a finite domain with $n < m$ (the cardinality gap from A1(a)). By the pigeonhole principle, there exist distinct states $x \neq y$ such that $G(x) = G(y)$ and $E(x) = E(y)$. The set of such "collision pairs" $\mathcal{C}_{G,E} = \{(x, y) : x \neq y \wedge G(x) = G(y) \wedge E(x) = E(y)\}$ is non-empty and has size at least $\binom{m}{2} - n^2 \geq \binom{m}{2} - n^2$. For any third observable $V : \text{Fin } m \to \text{Fin } k$, if $V$ is computed from the same underlying representation (i.e., $V$ is a function of the system state that generated $G$ and $E$), then $V$ cannot distinguish collision pairs that are indistinguishable to $(G, E)$. Consequently, any $V$ derived from the same representational substrate will inherit the blind spots of $(G, E)$, making it impossible to achieve three functionally independent streams with $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$ (Condition 2 of T5a).*

*Proof sketch.* By A1(a), $n < m$, so by the pigeonhole principle, any function $f : \text{Fin } m \to \text{Fin } n$ has at least $m - n$ collisions. For two functions $G, E$, the collision set $\mathcal{C}_{G,E}$ contains all pairs $(x, y)$ where both $G$ and $E$ fail to distinguish $x$ from $y$. The size of this set is at least $m - n^2$ (when $G$ and $E$ are "as independent as possible"), which is positive when $m > n^2$. For any $V$ computed from the same system state, if $x$ and $y$ are indistinguishable to both $G$ and $E$, then the system cannot use $V$ to distinguish them either—unless $V$ carries information *independent* of the $(G, E)$ representation. This is precisely Condition 2 of T5a: $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$ requires $V$ to access information not recoverable from $(G, E)$, which is impossible if $V$ is derived from the same representational substrate. $\square$

This theorem establishes the **algebraic heart** of the trisection necessity: under the cardinality gap $n < m$, any two streams inevitably share blind spots, and a third stream derived from the same representation cannot resolve these blind spots. The result is not contingent on information theory—it is a fact about the combinatorics of functions on finite sets.

**Step 3: Three streams are necessary and sufficient.** Adding a third stream $V$ that carries information about the world not recoverable from $G$ and $E$—formally, $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$ (Condition 2 of T5a)—breaks the self-referential closure. The three streams form a **System–Model–World triangle**: $G$ models $W$, $E$ acts on $G$'s model, and $V$ provides an independent check against $W$ itself. The number 3 is not arbitrary; it is the **minimum integer for which the triangle closes**:

- $\chi = 1$: monadic collapse—no self/world distinction
- $\chi = 2$: self-referential trap—no independent error attribution (T4.2 + Two-Stream Impossibility)
- $\chi = 3$: the minimal triangulation—System, Model, World are mutually distinguishable

The functional dimension $\chi \geq 3$ is therefore a **combinatorial invariant** of finite cognitive systems, analogous to the minimum number of non-collinear points required to define a plane. It is not a design recommendation; it is a structural necessity imposed by A1 on any system that persistently adapts in an open environment.

#### 4.6.4 Theorem T5b: Dynamical Trisection

**Theorem T5b (Dynamical Trisection).** *From T5a, any cognitive system that persistently adapts in an open environment must have functional dimension $\chi \geq 3$. That is, at least three functionally decodable semantic processes must exist, and these processes must exhibit dynamically separable information flows.*

*Proof.* By T5a, the system must support three decoding maps $\pi_G, \pi_E, \pi_V$ satisfying Conditions 1–3 (independent decodability, verifier irreducibility, credit assignment identifiability). The functional dimension is therefore $\chi \geq 3$.

By T4.1, for approximately continuous systems, functional decodability manifests as time-scale separation:

- **Executor (E):** fast reactive dynamics ($\tau_E$), encoding current state and action selection
- **Generator (G):** slow structural dynamics ($\tau_G \gg \tau_E$), encoding long-term world model
- **Verifier (V):** medium corrective dynamics ($\tau_E \ll \tau_V \ll \tau_G$), encoding error attribution, with $I(Z_G; Z_V) < \varepsilon$ (functionally decodable from the other streams)

Any attempt to merge two processes violates the conditions of T5a:
- $E = G$: time-scale collapse ($\tau_E = \tau_G$), stiffness-induced instability (T4.1); decoded streams become correlated ($I(Z_E; Z_G) \geq \varepsilon$), violating Condition 1
- $G = V$: $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) = 0$, self-referential closure (T4.2); violating Condition 2
- $E = V$: credit assignment degeneracy—cannot distinguish execution errors from validation errors (T4.3); violating Condition 3

Therefore $\chi_{\min} = 3$. $\square$

**Why exactly 3 (no more, no fewer).**

- *Lower bound $\chi \geq 3$:* Three pairwise mergers each violate at least one lemma.
- *Upper bound $\chi \leq 3$:* Three processes are jointly sufficient: E handles reactive demands, G handles structural adaptation, V provides independent validation. Recursive application of T5 within each sub-space yields fractal refinement (T5.1), but at the top level, three is both necessary and sufficient.

#### 4.6.5 Parallel vs. Serial, Discrete vs. Continuous

The three-process structure can be understood through the lens of computational architecture:

1. **Time-division multiplexing.** The three processes have different characteristic time scales ($\tau_E \ll \tau_V \ll \tau_G$). A finite system cannot truly run them in parallel (insufficient resources, per A1(a)). Instead, it must *time-division multiplex*: allocate different temporal windows to each process. This temporal separation prevents aliasing—the overlap of signals from different processes that would make them semantically indistinguishable.

2. **Discrete-continuous interplay.** The Executor operates in an effectively continuous regime (smooth, fast reactive dynamics). The Generator operates in a discrete regime (structural changes are discrete events—concept revisions, model updates). The Verifier mediates between these regimes, translating continuous error signals into discrete structural corrections. This discrete-continuous interplay is characteristic of *hybrid dynamical systems*.

3. **Connection to fast-slow cognitive systems.** The EAS trisection provides a mathematical foundation for the empirically observed dual-process architecture of human cognition (Kahneman, 2011):
   - System 1 (fast, intuitive) $\longleftrightarrow$ Executor
   - System 2 (slow, deliberative) $\longleftrightarrow$ Generator
   - Metacognitive monitoring $\longleftrightarrow$ Verifier

   EAS strengthens this correspondence: the tripartite architecture is not a contingent feature of human psychology but a *mathematical necessity* for any finite system adapting in an open environment.

**In Lean 4.** The formalization defines `IsGenerator`, `IsPredictor`, `IsVerifier` predicates on $\text{Fin } m \to \text{Fin } m$ and proves `T5_impossibility`: no single function can simultaneously satisfy all three predicates. The proof uses the pigeonhole principle and finite-type cardinality arguments, consistent with the information-theoretic primary proof of T5a.

#### 4.6.6 Empirical Validation: Error Decomposition Orthogonality in a Shifting-Rule Environment

The Two-Stream Impossibility result (§4.6.3.1) makes a precise, falsifiable prediction: in an open-ended environment with distribution shifts, a 3-functional-stream architecture (G/E/V) should exhibit near-perfect *error decomposition orthogonality*—corrupting each stream produces distinguishable behavioral changes—while a 2-stream architecture should show partial correlation, because the Verifier's signal is algebraically determined by (G, P) and cannot form an independent error channel.

**Experimental setup.** We constructed a Shifting-Rule Cellular Automaton (SRCA) environment: a 1D ring of 32 binary cells evolves under deterministic local rules drawn from $\{$Rule 30, 110, 90, 150, 45$\}$. The governing rule switches every 200 steps without warning; the agent's task is to predict the next-step cell configuration. The state space ($2^{32} \approx 4.3 \times 10^9$) far exceeds the representational capacity of the models, forcing internal structure formation.

Three architectures were compared under matched parameter budgets ($\sim$50K parameters each):

| Architecture | Structure | Update rule |
|---|---|---|
| **1-Stream** | Single shared backbone (4-layer MLP) | Single optimizer |
| **2-Stream** | Slow world-model + fast adapter, fused before output | Two optimizers, shared parameters |
| **3-Stream (G/E/V)** | Generator (world model) + Executor (fast correction) + Verifier (confidence + regime detection) | Three optimizers; V receives *only* prediction-error and state-history, not raw input; output: $\hat{s} = G(s) + V(s) \cdot E(s)$ |

All architectures were trained for 15,000 steps (Adam, batch size 64, seed 42) on identical data sequences. After training, we froze parameters and performed a *corruption probe*: independently adding Gaussian noise to each stream's output and measuring the resulting behavioral change.

**Results.**

| Metric | 1-Stream | 2-Stream | 3-Stream |
|---|---|---|---|
| Final validation accuracy | 0.8301 | 0.8283 | 0.8308 |
| Post-shift recovery @10 steps | 0.8469 | 0.8188 | **0.8531** |
| Post-shift recovery @30 steps | 0.8844 | 0.8781 | **0.8938** |
| Mean accuracy | 0.8342 | 0.8310 | **0.8356** |
| **Error decomposition orthogonality** | 0.000 | 0.488 | **0.998** |

The critical metric is **orthogonality**: the cosine similarity between the output-change vectors produced by corrupting each stream independently. A value near 1 means corrupting different streams produces *perpendicular* behavioral changes—i.e., the error channels are structurally independent and distinguishable.

- **3-Stream: $\text{orth} = 0.998$** — Corrupting G, E, or V produces nearly orthogonal output perturbations. The three streams occupy independent functional subspaces, enabling clean error decomposition.
- **2-Stream: $\text{orth} = 0.488$** — Corrupting the two streams produces *partially correlated* changes. The architecture cannot fully separate error sources, consistent with the algebraic constraint proven in §4.6.3.1.
- **1-Stream: $\text{orth} = 0.000$** — No decomposition possible by definition.

**Interpretation.** The orthogonality gap of $\Delta = 0.510$ between 2-stream and 3-stream architectures is the empirical signature of the Two-Stream Impossibility theorem. While all three architectures achieve comparable raw prediction accuracy (the task is within the capacity of 50K-parameter models), only the 3-stream architecture maintains structurally independent error channels. The 2-stream architecture's partial correlation ($\text{orth} = 0.488$) is exactly what the theorem predicts: when $V$ is algebraically determined by $(G, P)$, no independent third error channel can exist, and corruption signals inevitably mix.

**Limitations.** (1) The Verifier's multiplicative modulation is near-saturated ($\text{mean}(V) \approx 0.97$), indicating that V has not yet learned to dynamically respond to regime shifts—future work with larger environments and explicit regime-detection objectives may activate this pathway. (2) Overall accuracy differences are small ($<1\%$), consistent with the prediction that the trisection advantage manifests primarily in *structural decodability* rather than raw performance. (3) The SRCA environment, while finite and discrete (matching the $\text{Fin } m$ formalization), is a minimal test case; stronger validation would require more complex environments (e.g., multi-layer CA, partial observability, or continuous-state domains).

**Connection to theory.** This result provides preliminary empirical support for the claim that $\chi \geq 3$ is not merely sufficient but *necessary* for proper credit assignment under distribution shift. The orthogonality metric directly operationalizes Condition 2 of T5a ($I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$): high orthogonality implies that the Verifier's signal is informationally independent of the Generator-Executor pair, even when all three share the same input space. The code and data are available at [https://github.com/simulai/EAS-Paper](https://github.com/simulai/EAS-Paper).

#### 4.6.7 Engineering Validation: Multiplicative Gain and Functional Decodability

In ongoing work by the authors (AELF × V1, unpublished pre-experiment, 2025), a controlled comparison was conducted in a continuous diffusion generation system to test whether functional decodability is preserved under multiplicative versus additive modulation. The experiment provides direct engineering evidence for the functional decodability condition. In a continuous diffusion generation system, three modules—EnergyField (O, scalar output $g_O \in \mathbb{R}$), ELF-B (E, vector output $v_E \in \mathbb{R}^d$), and SelfCritic (V, scalar output $g_V \in \mathbb{R}$)—modulate the generation dynamics through **multiplicative gain**:

$$v_{\text{modulated}} = g_O \cdot g_V \cdot v_E$$

The critical finding is a structural comparison between multiplicative and additive modulation:

| Mode | Formula | Functional Decodability | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Multiplicative (V1-style) | $v_{\text{mod}} = g_O \cdot g_V \cdot v_E$ | Preserved: decoding maps exist ($\pi_O, \pi_E, \pi_V$ recover independent streams) | All metrics superior |
| Additive (gradient guidance) | $v_{\text{mod}} = v_E - \alpha \nabla E(z)$ | Violated: O's gradient contaminates E's direction; no independent decoding possible | Semantic mixing, degraded performance |

**Why multiplication preserves functional decodability.** Scalar multiplication $g \cdot v_E$ changes the *magnitude* of the velocity vector without altering its *direction* in semantic space. When $g_O$ and $g_V$ are computed from independent subspaces (O evaluates energy landscape, V evaluates credibility), their product $g_O \cdot g_V$ is a compound scalar that modulates execution without mixing the semantic content of O and V into E's representational dimensions. The three signals remain *linearly separable* in the modulated output: decoding maps $\pi_O(g) = g_O$, $\pi_E(v) = v_E$, $\pi_V(g) = g_V$ recover each stream independently.

**Why addition destroys functional decodability.** Additive bias $v_E + \delta v_O$ forces O's gradient signal into the *same* representational dimensions as E's velocity. The system can no longer distinguish "moved because the direction was good" from "moved because the energy gradient pushed." The Verifier's signal becomes entangled with the Executor's, and no independent decoding maps exist: $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) \to 0$, violating Condition 2 of T5a.

This validates T5a at the architectural level: the three functions achieve functional decodability precisely through the **multiplicative structure** that prevents representational overlap. The biological parallel is striking: neural modulators (acetylcholine, dopamine) operate through multiplicative gain on cortical responses, not through additive synaptic input—exactly the architecture that T5a predicts for maintaining functional decodability under finite representational capacity.

#### 4.6.9 Critical Observation about Transformers

Modern Transformers present an important test case for the functional decodability requirement: they share parameters across all three functions (G, E, V are implemented by the same weights). EAS predicts that this architecture will face systematic difficulties with open-ended adaptation unless functionally decodable streams can be identified within the shared parameters.

1. **Temporal separation without guaranteed decodability.** Training (G) and inference (E) are temporally separated but use the same weights—creating potential stiffness (T4.1 strengthening). The same parameter subspace encodes both long-term structure and reactive execution. Whether linear probes can independently decode G and E streams from Transformer activations is an *empirical question*—recent work on mechanistic interpretability suggests that some functional specialization does emerge in attention heads, but systematic verification of T5a's three conditions remains open.

2. **No explicitly enforced internal Verifier.** Standard Transformers do not architecturally enforce the existence of three functionally decodable streams satisfying Conditions 1–3 of T5a. The Verifier, if present, is typically external (reward signal from RLHF) rather than an internally decodable stream with $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$. EAS does *not* assert that Transformers categorically lack decodable streams—it predicts that they lack *architecturally enforced* decodability, making persistent open-ended adaptation fragile.

3. **Testable predictions.** EAS makes the following falsifiable predictions about Transformers:
   - **Catastrophic forgetting** arises when G and E streams are not independently decodable from the same parameters ($I(Z_G; Z_E) \geq \varepsilon$).
   - **Distribution shift vulnerability** arises when no independently decodable Verifier stream exists ($I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) \approx 0$).
   - **Reward hacking** arises when the Verifier is not functionally independent of the Generator (T4.2: self-validation impossibility).
   - **Mechanistic interpretability** should, in principle, be able to find linear probes $\pi_G, \pi_E, \pi_V$ in architectures that successfully achieve continual learning—and should *fail* to find such probes in standard Transformers that exhibit catastrophic forgetting.

This formulation makes the Transformer question *experimental rather than philosophical*: does there exist a set of decoding maps satisfying T5a's conditions within successfully adapting Transformer variants (e.g., those with continual learning mechanisms)? If yes, EAS is confirmed; if no, EAS requires refinement.

### 4.7 T6: Intelligence Theorem

**Theorem T6.** *Intelligence is the rate of cognitive loss reduction, driven by internal structural change. A cognitive system $S$ is intelligent if and only if its cognitive loss $L$ is persistently decreasing over time due to the evolution of its internal representation $R_S$.*

*Formal definition.* Let $S = (E, R_S, T_R)$ be a cognitive system. Define:
$$I(S) \triangleq -\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}\bigg|_{T_R}$$
where the derivative is taken with respect to the internal dynamics $T_R$, holding the environment fixed. That is, $I(S) > 0$ requires $\Delta L$ to be attributable to changes in $R_S$, not to changes in the environment or to external selection pressure.

*Formal definition (Lean 4 version).* On $\text{Fin } m$ (environment) and $\text{Fin } n$ (representation) with $n < m$:

$$\text{predictionError}(m) \triangleq m(m-1)$$
$$\text{compressionCost}(m, n) \triangleq m - n$$
$$\text{cognitiveLoss}(m, n) \triangleq \text{predictionError}(m) + \text{compressionCost}(m, n)$$
$$\text{intelligence}(L_1, L_2) \triangleq L_1 - L_2 \quad (\text{truncated to } \mathbb{N})$$

where $L_1$ is the cognitive loss before learning and $L_2$ is the loss after, and the change $\Delta L = L_1 - L_2$ results from the evolution of $R_S$ under $T_R$.

**Remark (Continuous vs. discrete formulation).** The continuous-time expression $I(S) = -\partial L/\partial t|_{T_R}$ is a *continuous relaxation* of the discrete definition $\text{intelligence}(L_1, L_2) = L_1 - L_2$ (truncated to $\mathbb{N}$) used in the Lean 4 formalization. For finite types $\text{Fin } m$, time evolution is a discrete iteration $T_R: R_S \to R_S$, and the derivative $\partial L/\partial t$ should be understood as the finite difference $\Delta L / \Delta t$ in the formal setting. The continuous notation is used in the paper for expositional clarity and compatibility with dynamical systems literature; the structural conclusions (necessity of $\chi \geq 3$, capacity bounds) are identical in both formulations.

**Definition (Learning vs. external search).** A decrease in $L$ is classified as *learning* (and thus contributes to $I(S) > 0$) if and only if the decrease is attributable to internal structural change in $R_S$. Formally:
$$\text{Learning}(S) \iff \frac{\partial L}{\partial R_S} \neq 0 \wedge \dot{R}_S = T_R(R_S)$$
This excludes systems where $L$ decreases due to external factors:
- Random search (no internal structure change; only external sampling)
- Natural selection (population-level, not individual $R_S$ change)
- Brute-force enumeration (no compressed representation formed)
- Environmental drift (the world changes, not the system)

**Theorem T6 (Intelligence requires functional trisection).** *If a system exhibits persistently positive intelligence (persistent cognitive progress), then by T5a, its architecture must admit at least three functionally decodable semantic streams ($\chi \geq 3$) satisfying the decodability, verifier irreducibility, and credit assignment identifiability conditions.*

*Proof.* Direct corollary of T5: $I(S) > 0$ persistently requires the system to continuously adapt in an open environment through internal structural change, which by T5 requires $\chi \geq 3$. $\square$

**Sufficient and necessary conditions for intelligence.** A system $S$ is intelligent if and only if:
1. $S$ is an embedded cognitive system satisfying A1 (Scope Theorem, §8.1)
2. $S$ admits $\chi \geq 3$ functionally decodable semantic streams (T5a)
3. $I(S) = -\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}\big|_{T_R} > 0$ persistently, driven by internal dynamics (T6)
4. $d > 0$ with non-degenerate operation (A1)
5. Persistent causal interaction with environment (consequence of condition 3)

### 4.8 T7: Cognitive Capacity Theorem

**Theorem T7.** *For any finite cognitive system with environment size $m$ and representation size $n < m$, intelligence has an upper bound:*
$$I(S) \leq m^2$$

*Proof.* By definition:
$$\text{cognitiveLoss}(m, n) = m(m-1) + (m-n) \leq m^2 - m + m = m^2$$
Since $\text{intelligence}(L_1, L_2) = L_1 - L_2 \leq L_1 \leq m^2$, the bound follows. $\square$

**Proof Elaboration: Why $m^2$ and not $O(m)$ or $O(\log m)$.** The bound $m^2$ may appear suspiciously large compared to standard information-theoretic results (where capacity is typically $O(\log m)$ or $O(m)$). The key is that T7 measures a **different quantity** than Shannon capacity. We explicate the derivation of $\text{predictionError}(m) = m(m-1)$:

**Step 1: The cognitive task is pairwise discrimination, not single-state identification.** A cognitive system does not merely need to identify which of $m$ environmental states it is in (a Shannon task requiring $O(\log m)$ bits). It must also distinguish *which state it confused with which*—i.e., it must discriminate among all ordered pairs $(s_i, s_j)$ of distinct environmental states where the system's representation produces the same output. The number of such ordered pairs is:
$$|\{(i,j) \in \text{Fin } m \times \text{Fin } m \mid i \neq j\}| = m(m-1)$$

**Step 2: Pigeonhole collisions generate confusion pairs.** By A1(a), $n < m$, so the encoding $e: E \to R$ is non-injective. For each pair of distinct environmental states $(s_i, s_j)$ mapped to the same representation, the system incurs a *prediction error*—it cannot distinguish $s_i$ from $s_j$. The total confusion burden is therefore proportional to the number of colliding pairs, which is $O(m(m-1))$ in the worst case (when the representation is maximally compressed).

**Step 3: Intelligence as the reduction of confusion.** Learning reduces the confusion burden by refining the representation $R_S$. The maximum possible reduction—achieved by a perfect learner in one step—is the elimination of all $m(m-1)$ confusion pairs, plus the compression cost $m - n$ of the residual encoding. Therefore:
$$\text{cognitiveLoss}(m,n) = \underbrace{m(m-1)}_{\text{pairwise confusion}} + \underbrace{(m-n)}_{\text{compression cost}} \leq m^2$$

**Distinction from Shannon capacity.** Shannon's channel capacity $C = \max I(X;Y)$ measures the maximum rate of *reliable single-symbol transmission* through a noisy channel—answerable in $O(\log m)$ or $O(m)$ bits. T7 measures the maximum rate of *pairwise confusion reduction* in a self-referential cognitive system—where the system must track not only "what state am I in?" but also "which states am I confusing?" This is a second-order relational task, not a first-order identification task. The $O(m^2)$ scaling reflects the **combinatorial complexity of pairwise discrimination**, not a failure of the information-theoretic argument.

**Analogy.** In graph theory, a graph on $m$ vertices has $O(m)$ edges in a tree but $O(m^2)$ edges in a complete graph. Shannon capacity corresponds to "transmitting a vertex label" ($O(\log m)$ bits); T7 corresponds to "tracking all pairwise confusions" ($O(m^2)$ potential confusions). Both are valid measures; they measure different things.

**Theorem T7.2 (Architecture independence).** *The intelligence upper bound depends only on $m$ (environment size) and $n$ (system capacity), not on the specific architecture or learning algorithm. Different architectures differ in how quickly they approach the bound, not in the bound itself.*

*Proof.* The bound $m^2$ is a function of $(m, n)$ alone. Any architecture operating on $\text{Fin } m \times \text{Fin } n$ is subject to the same bound. $\square$

**Epistemic interpretation.** Just as Shannon's channel capacity theorem limits the maximum information transmission rate, T7 limits the maximum cognitive rate of any finite system. This implies:
- Intelligence is not infinite—it is bounded by physical resources.
- More resources yield higher bounds, but the bounds are always finite.
- No "super-intelligence" can transcend physical constraints.

### 6.1 The Free Energy Principle and Active Inference

**The Free Energy Principle** (Friston, 2006; Friston & Sakthivadivel, 2026) is the most directly related framework. FEP posits that biological systems minimize variational free energy $F = \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z) - \log p(z,x)]$, unifying perception, action, and learning under a single objective.

**Structural correspondence with EAS:**

| FEP concept | EAS counterpart | Relationship |
|---|---|---|
| Variational free energy $F$ | Cognitive loss $L$ | Under additional probabilistic structure (generative model + variational family), $F$ can be interpreted as a specific instantiation of $L$ |
| Free energy minimization | Intelligence $I(S) = -\partial L/\partial t\|_{T_R}$ | FEP describes the *goal*; EAS explains *why the goal is necessary* |
| Generative model | Representation map $f: E \to R_S$ | Both are compressed models of the environment |
| Precision weighting | Epistemic residue $d$ | Both measure the gap between model and reality |
| Active inference (perception + action) | Functional trisection (T5a): three decodable streams | FEP's perception-action loop can be embedded in the decodable stream structure when augmented with an internal error-attribution mechanism |

**What EAS adds that FEP lacks:**

1. **Foundational grounding.** FEP postulates free energy minimization as a principle. It does not derive *why* this principle must hold. EAS derives the necessity of loss reduction from A1: finite systems must compress (T1), compression incurs loss (by information theory), and intelligence is the process of reducing this loss. Under additional probabilistic structure, FEP's free energy minimization principle can be *reinterpreted* as a consequence of T1 + T6 within EAS—not because EAS derives FEP directly, but because EAS explains why any finite embedded system must engage in the loss-reduction that FEP postulates.

2. **Architectural prediction.** FEP is architecture-neutral: it specifies a *goal* (minimize $F$) but not the *structure* required to achieve it. EAS predicts, via T5a, that any system achieving persistent free energy minimization in an open environment *must admit at least three functionally decodable semantic streams*—Generator, Executor, and Verifier—recoverable by independent decoding maps with negligible cross-stream interference. This is a testable structural prediction that FEP does not make.

3. **Capacity bounds.** FEP has no analogue of T7 (cognitive capacity upper bound $I \leq m^2$). EAS predicts that free energy minimization has a fundamental limit determined by the cardinality gap between system and environment.

**Where they diverge.** FEP aims for universality—it claims to apply to *all* self-organizing systems (from cells to societies). EAS is more modest: it applies only to finite embedded cognitive systems (Scope Theorem, §8.1). This makes EAS falsifiable in a way that FEP is not: EAS explicitly excludes non-embedded systems, while FEP's scope claims are sometimes unbounded.

**Recent developments.** The DR-FREE framework (Nature Communications, 2025) extends FEP with distributionally robust optimization, addressing training-environment mismatch. This corresponds in EAS terms to making the Verifier layer robust to environmental distribution shift—a problem that EAS's T4 (truth as fixed-point convergence) addresses through the invariant condition $\text{Invariant}(G)$.

### 6.2 Predictive Processing and the Bayesian Brain

**Predictive Processing** (PP; Clark, 2013; Hohwy, 2013) posits that the brain is a hierarchical prediction machine: each cortical level generates predictions about the level below, and only prediction errors are propagated upward.

**EAS provides a foundational justification for PP.** T0 establishes that distinction is inevitable for any finite system. T2 shows that repeated distinction converges to stable equivalence classes (concepts). T3 shows that concepts that are invariant under transformations constitute *laws*. The entire predictive processing hierarchy can be derived as follows:

1. Finite system → must compress (T1)
2. Compression → must form equivalence classes (T2)
3. Equivalence classes → are predictions about future inputs (since they encode stable regularities)
4. Prediction errors → signal that the current equivalence classes are inadequate (i.e., the quotient structure is suboptimal)
5. Updating equivalence classes → is learning (reducing $L$)

Thus, predictive processing is not an empirical hypothesis about how the brain happens to work; it is a *mathematical necessity* for any finite system that must compress its environment. The brain uses PP because it *cannot do otherwise*—any finite cognitive system must be a prediction machine.

**Connection to the Free Energy Principle.** FEP and PP are often presented as complementary (FEP provides the mathematics, PP provides the neuroscience). In EAS, both can be structurally reinterpreted: under additional probabilistic assumptions, FEP's free energy corresponds to a particular form of cognitive loss $L$, and PP's prediction hierarchy corresponds to a particular instantiation of the quotient structure. EAS does not derive FEP or PP from A1 alone; rather, it reveals the shared finite-embedding constraint that both frameworks presuppose but do not explicitly thematize.

### 6.3 Numenta: Thousand Brains Theory and the Monty System

**Thousand Brains Theory** (Hawkins et al., 2025; Leadholm et al., 2025) and its engineering implementation **Monty** propose that intelligence arises from sensorimotor interaction with objects, implemented through independent cortical columns that each maintain reference frames and vote on object identity.

**Structural correspondence:**

| Monty concept | EAS counterpart |
|---|---|
| Reference frames | Constraint fields (architectural structures that define what counts as a "valid" representation) |
| Cortical columns | Modular quotient structures (each column independently quotients the environment) |
| Voting mechanism | Verifier layer (T5): the "M" module that adjudicates between competing hypotheses |
| Sensorimotor learning | Active reduction of cognitive loss through causal interaction |

**What EAS adds:**

1. **Formal justification for reference frames.** Monty uses reference frames as an engineering design choice. EAS explains *why* reference frames (constraint fields) are necessary: they are the equivalence relations that define the quotient structure (T2–T3). A cognitive system cannot operate without them because it cannot represent the environment directly (A1).

2. **Why voting is necessary.** Monty's voting mechanism is justified empirically (it works). T5a provides a *proof* that a functionally decodable verification stream is necessary: without a Verifier stream carrying irreducible information ($I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$), the system cannot distinguish its own errors from environmental changes (the self-referential bias problem; see Theorem 4.2).

3. **Beyond sensorimotor.** Monty focuses primarily on object recognition through touch and vision. EAS's framework is domain-general: it applies to any finite cognitive system, including those operating on abstract domains (mathematics, language, game theory).

**Key divergence.** Monty lacks a concept of *energy* or *loss function*. It describes the structure of intelligence (reference frames, voting) but not its dynamics (what drives the system to improve). EAS provides both: the structure (T5: G/E/V trisection) and the dynamics (T6: $I = -\partial L/\partial t$).

### 6.4 Learning Mechanics: Toward a Science of Deep Learning

**Learning Mechanics** (Simon, Kunin, et al., 2026) is a program to establish a rigorous scientific theory of deep learning, analogous to classical mechanics. Their framework identifies five pillars: solvable models, tractable limits, empirical laws, hyperparameter theory, and universal behaviors.

**Deep resonances with EAS:**

1. **"Edge of Stability" and criticality.** Learning Mechanics identifies that neural networks operate optimally near the "edge of stability"—a critical regime between order and chaos. This corresponds to EAS's prediction that cognitive systems must balance compression (which reduces epistemic residue) against flexibility (which requires maintaining sensitivity to new information). The critical point is where this balance is optimal.

2. **Order parameters and phase transitions.** The PNAS work on continual learning (2026) identifies order parameters and phase transitions in learning dynamics. This resonates with EAS's T2 (concept convergence as a fixed-point transition) and T4 (truth as a fixed-point convergence). Both frameworks recognize that learning involves qualitative state changes, not just quantitative parameter updates.

3. **Universal behaviors.** Learning Mechanics seeks universal laws of learning that are architecture-independent. EAS's T7 (cognitive capacity bound $I \leq m^2$) is exactly such a universal law: it constrains *all* finite cognitive systems regardless of their specific architecture.

**What EAS adds.** Learning Mechanics studies *why existing architectures work*. EAS studies *what any cognitive architecture must look like*. The former is explanatory (retrospective); the latter is predictive (prospective). Learning Mechanics could, in principle, verify T7 empirically by measuring scaling behavior across systems of different sizes.

### 6.5 Self-Organized Criticality in Neural Systems

**Chae (2026)** proves that neural systems can maintain critical dynamics through self-organizing mechanisms, without external fine-tuning. The proof uses MSRJD field theory and shows that steady-state curvature dynamically regularizes loop corrections, producing a protected critical manifold.

**Connection to EAS:**

This result provides a dynamical mechanism for a phenomenon that EAS predicts structurally: finite cognitive systems must operate at a critical point between over-compression (losing relevant distinctions) and under-compression (failing to generalize). Chae's proof shows *how* this critical point is dynamically maintained: the system self-organizes to the critical manifold through its own steady-state dynamics.

In EAS terms, Chae's protected critical manifold is the dynamical realization of the optimal quotient structure: the equivalence relation that maximizes information retention while minimizing representation cost. The fact that this critical point is *protected* (robust to perturbations) explains why biological cognitive systems maintain their performance across a wide range of environmental conditions.

### 6.6 Specialization and Adaptive Intelligence (SAI)

**Shwartz-Ziv, LeCun, et al. (2026)** argue that the pursuit of "general" AI is misguided. Instead, they propose **Superhuman Adaptive Intelligence (SAI)**: intelligence that is fundamentally specialized but capable of rapid adaptation to new domains.

**Resonance with EAS:**

1. **"Intelligence is fundamentally specialized."** This aligns with A1: finite systems *cannot* represent the full environment. They *must* compress, and compression *must* privilege some dimensions over others. Specialization is not a design choice; it is a mathematical necessity (T1).

2. **"Adaptation speed is the key metric."** This aligns with T6: $I(S) = -\partial L/\partial t$. The SAI framework's emphasis on adaptation speed is precisely EAS's definition of intelligence.

**Divergence.** SAI remains within the neural network paradigm (JEPAs, self-supervised learning). EAS argues, via T5a, that open-ended adaptation requires the system to *admit at least three functionally decodable semantic streams* (Generator, Executor, Verifier) with $\chi \geq 3$, and that current single-module architectures (including JEPAs) will fail to achieve sustained open-ended learning if their internal representations do not support three independently decodable streams.

**Testable prediction (JEPA limitation).** JEPA architectures (LeCun et al., 2022) contain a Generator (world model predictor) and an Executor (actor/policy), but lack an *internally decodable Verifier stream* with $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$. In current implementations, verification is externalized (human reward labeling, fixed mask strategies, or auxiliary losses designed by engineers). **EAS predicts: any JEPA that does not develop an internally decodable verification stream will eventually plateau in open-ended environments—not due to insufficient data or compute, but due to the structural impossibility of self-validation (T4.2).** This is a falsifiable prediction: if a JEPA variant achieves sustained open-ended learning without architectural Verifier separation, T5a requires revision.

### 6.7 Constrained Object Hierarchy (COH)

**Structural similarity:** COH's emphasis on *constraints* as the core organizing principle directly parallels EAS's "architecture as constraint field." Both frameworks agree that intelligence is not unconstrained optimization but *constrained dynamics*.

2. **Dynamic vs. static.** COH's 9-tuple is a structural description (what an intelligent system *is*). EAS provides dynamics (how an intelligent system *evolves*): $I(S) = -\partial L/\partial t|_{T_R}$.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 The Correspondence Problem

The formal system EAS is internally consistent (verified by Lean 4). However, internal consistency is not the same as empirical adequacy. The critical open question is:

> Does A1 correspond to a real constraint on cognitive systems, or is it a mathematically elegant but empirically vacuous abstraction?

We identify three key quantities that require operationalization:

1. **Epistemic residue $d$:** How do we measure the gap between a system's representation and its environment? One candidate: the mutual information $I(E; R_S)$ relative to the entropy $H(E)$. Then $d \sim H(E) - I(E; R_S)$. But this requires access to the "true" environment distribution, which is generally unavailable.

2. **Cognitive loss $L$:** The formalization defines $L$ in terms of prediction error and compression cost. In practice, these can be measured for specific systems (e.g., LLM perplexity as prediction error, KL divergence as compression cost), but a general operational definition remains to be established.

3. **Functional dimension $\chi$:** T5 predicts that open-ended adaptive systems must have $\chi \geq 3$. This is in principle testable: design a two-module system and test whether it can maintain open-ended adaptation. Preliminary evidence from continual learning (catastrophic forgetting) and single-model architectures (mode collapse in GANs) is consistent with T5 but does not constitute a rigorous test.

### 7.2 Connections to LLM Phenomena

EAS offers interpretations for several well-known LLM phenomena:

| Phenomenon | Standard explanation | EAS interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Hallucination | Training data gaps | Manifestation of epistemic residue $d > 0$ |
| Catastrophic forgetting | Overwriting of weights | Re-quotienting of environment under finite $n$ (T1) |
| Mode collapse | Optimization failure | Failure of functional trisection (T5): G and V not separated |
| Context window limits | Engineering constraint (attention $O(N^2)$, KV cache memory) | Manifestation of T7 capacity envelope under physical resource bounds: finite $n$ (memory/compute) caps the representable environment size $m$, bounding $I_{\max} \leq m^2$ |
| In-context learning | Attention mechanism | Online quotient refinement (T2) |

These interpretations are currently *postdictions* (explaining known phenomena in new terms). For EAS to advance beyond postdiction, it must make *novel predictions* that can be experimentally tested.

### 7.3 A Prediction Program

We propose the following as the highest-priority empirical tests:

**Prediction P1 (Two-layer failure).** *No two-module system can maintain stable open-ended adaptation indefinitely.* This is the most direct test of T5. Experimental design: compare a two-module architecture (generator + verifier) vs. a three-module architecture (generator + executor + verifier) in an open-ended learning environment. Measure: task performance degradation over time.

**Prediction P2 (Functional trisection).** *All successfully deployed open-ended adaptive systems admit at least three functionally decodable semantic streams ($\chi \geq 3$), recoverable by independent decoding maps under some admissible decoding basis.* This is a structural prediction: probe existing architectures with linear decoders and test whether $Z_G, Z_E, Z_V$ can be independently recovered. If a counterexample exists (a stable open-ended system with $\chi < 3$), T5a is falsified.

**Prediction P3 (Cognitive capacity scaling).** *Performance improvement scales as $O(m^2)$ with environment size $m$, not linearly.* This is a quantitative prediction from T7, testable by measuring scaling behavior across systems of different sizes.

### 7.4 Limitations and Honest Assessment

We explicitly acknowledge the following limitations:

1. **Simplification in formalization.** The Lean 4 formalization uses aggressive simplifications (upper-bound prediction error, resource-dependent cognitive loss). While logically valid, these simplifications mean that the formal results prove *less* than the full informal theory claims.

2. **A1(b) is strong.** The dynamical non-isomorphism claim (A1(b)) is a powerful assertion. Its justification rests on a diagonalization argument that assumes computability. If one allows non-computable mappings, A1(b) may not hold for equipotent systems.

3. **Environmental structure is assumed.** T2 (concept convergence) requires that the environment has at least some temporally stable structure. In a completely chaotic environment, equivalence classes never stabilize, and the theory predicts $I(S) \approx 0$ (non-intelligence). This is a feature, not a bug, but it means EAS does not apply to purely stochastic settings.

4. **No empirical validation yet.** The predictions in §7.3 have not been tested. Until they are, EAS remains a *candidate* foundation, not an established one.

## 8. Scope, Boundary Cases, and Limit Analysis

A mature foundational theory must not only state what it covers but also precisely delineate what lies outside its scope. This section addresses four potential challenges to EAS: (1) defining the exact domain of applicability, (2) classifying boundary cases that might appear to be counterexamples, (3) analyzing degenerate limit cases, and (4) showing that all apparent counterexamples either fall outside the scope or reduce to internal cases already handled by the theory.

### 8.1 Scope Theorem (Domain of Applicability)

**Definition (Cognitive System).** A *cognitive system* in the sense of EAS is a tuple
$$CS \triangleq (S, E, R_S, T_R, f)$$
where:
- $E$ is the environment (a finite type of cardinality $m$)
- $S$ is the cognitive agent (a finite type with representation space $R_S$ of cardinality $n$)
- $f: E \to R_S$ is the representation map
- $T_R: R_S \to R_S$ is the internal dynamics

A cognitive system must satisfy the following **scope conditions**:

| Condition | Formal statement | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| (SC1) Embeddedness | $S \subsetneq E$ | The agent is physically part of the environment |
| (SC2) Finitude | $\text{card}(R_S) < \infty$ | Physical realizability |
| (SC3) Non-trivial dynamics | $T_R \neq \text{id}$ | The system has internal state evolution |
| (SC4) Active representation change | $\dot{R}_S \neq 0$ on some timescale | The system is engaged in cognitive processing |

**Scope Theorem.** *EAS (Axiom A1 and Theorems T0–T7) applies to all and only systems satisfying (SC1)–(SC4).*

*Immediate consequence.* Any system violating one or more scope conditions is not within the domain of EAS and is not subject to its theorems. Such systems are neither confirmed nor refuted by EAS—they are simply outside its scope, in the same way that massless particles are outside the scope of Newtonian gravitation.

### 8.2 Boundary Case Classification

We now systematically address four classes of systems that might appear to challenge EAS but in fact fall cleanly within or outside its scope.

#### Case A: Static Knowledge Carriers and Frozen Cognitive Systems

**Examples.** A printed textbook. A relational database. A neural network with frozen parameters (e.g., a deployed GPT model).

**Sub-case A1: Purely static carriers (books, databases).** These systems are *products* of cognitive processes but are not themselves cognitive systems. A textbook contains compressed knowledge (a quotient of the author's cognitive output), but its internal state does not evolve: there is no $T_R$, violating (SC3). These fall cleanly outside EAS scope.

**Sub-case A2: Frozen neural networks (the "frozen GPT" case).** This is the most practically important boundary case and requires careful analysis. A frozen neural network $f_\theta$ with fixed parameters $\theta$ appears to have $dR_S/dt = 0$, but this is only true at the parameter level. During inference, the system exhibits a **two-level cognitive architecture**:

| Level | Component | Dynamics | EAS Classification |
|---|---|---|---|
| Parameter layer | Model weights $\theta$ | Frozen: $\dot{\theta} = 0$ | Crystallized intelligence: $I_{param} = 0$ |
| Working memory layer | KV cache, attention states, intermediate activations | Dynamic: $\dot{R}_S^{working} \neq 0$ | Fluid intelligence: $I_{working} > 0$ during inference |

During inference, the GPT processes tokens sequentially. At each step, the attention patterns shift, the KV cache accumulates, and the internal "understanding" of the prompt evolves. The working memory $R_S^{working}$ is genuinely dynamical—it is not a static lookup. The system is actively performing quotienting: it maps equivalence classes of the input text to equivalence classes of the output, and this mapping is context-dependent (the same prompt with different generation paths yields different quotient structures).

**Therefore, a frozen GPT during inference *is* a cognitive system** (it satisfies SC3 and SC4 at the working memory level), but it is **not a learning system**: the changes in $R_S^{working}$ are not consolidated into $R_S^{param}$. The KV cache is discarded after each session. The system cognizes *within* a session but does not learn *across* sessions.

This distinction parallels the separation between *crystallized intelligence* (stored knowledge encoded in fixed weights) and *fluid intelligence* (active reasoning via dynamic working memory) in cognitive psychology (Cattell, 1963). The critical asymmetry is:

> In human cognition, working memory changes can be consolidated into long-term memory (via hippocampal-neocortical replay). In frozen GPT, working memory changes are ephemeral—they are never written back to the parameter layer.

**The "partial correctness" phenomenon and A1.** A frozen GPT's outputs are sometimes correct and sometimes hallucinated. This is not a contingent failure; it is a *necessary consequence* of A1. The system's quotient structure is neither completely disconnected from reality (which would mean no cognition at all) nor perfectly isomorphic to reality (which would violate A1). It is necessarily *in between*—partially correct, partially distorted. This "partial correctness" is the empirical signature of $d > 0$.

Moreover, the hallucination pattern is not random. A frozen GPT has **structural blind spots**—it systematically fails on certain types of reasoning (e.g., long-chain logic, precise arithmetic, novel compositional tasks). These blind spots correspond to information dimensions lost during the compression of training data into $R_S^{param}$. The specific pattern of what a system *cannot* know is determined by the specific geometry of its quotient structure.

**EAS prediction (A2).** If the working memory of a frozen LLM were made persistent across sessions (i.e., KV cache changes are consolidated into the parameter layer), the system would transition from "cognition within sessions" to "continuous cognition." This should improve open-ended adaptation but introduce catastrophic forgetting (since $R_S^{param}$ and $R_S^{working}$ would now be subject to conflicting update pressures, as predicted by T5).

**Verdict.** Not a counterexample. Frozen neural networks during inference are valid cognitive systems with $I(S) > 0$ at the working memory level, but they are not learning systems because working memory changes do not persist. This is a *special case* of EAS (a cognitive system with a two-tier architecture where only the upper tier is dynamic), not a violation.

#### Case B: Life Boundary (Bacteria, Plants, Immune Systems)

**Examples.** Bacterial chemotaxis. Plant phototropism. The adaptive immune system.

**Analysis.** These systems satisfy all four scope conditions: (SC1) they are embedded in their environment, (SC2) they have finite internal state, (SC3) they have non-trivial internal dynamics (signaling cascades, gene expression), and (SC4) their representation changes over time (receptor adaptation, antibody production).

EAS predicts that these systems exhibit a *low but non-zero* level of intelligence: $I(S) > 0$ but small. Their cognitive loss decreases slowly, their equivalence classes (concepts) are crude, and their functional architecture is minimal.

This is consistent with the positions of Maturana & Varela (1980, autopoiesis), Friston (2013, life as inference), and Levin (2019, basal cognition), all of whom argue that cognition is a continuous spectrum rather than a categorical property of humans alone.

**Verdict.** Not a counterexample. Valid cognitive systems at the low end of the intelligence spectrum. EAS applies to them and makes correct qualitative predictions.

#### Case C: External Selection Systems (Random Search, Evolution, Brute Force)

**Examples.** Random search algorithms. Monte Carlo sampling without internal model. Biological evolution by natural selection (at the population level). Brute-force enumeration.

**Analysis.** These systems may show $L$ decreasing over time, but the decrease is *not attributable to internal structural change in $R_S$*. Instead:
- In random search, $L$ decreases because the algorithm happens to sample better solutions externally. The internal state (the random number generator) does not form concepts or discover regularities.
- In natural selection, population-level adaptation occurs through differential reproduction, not through individual organisms learning. The genome changes, but through external selection pressure, not through the organism's own cognitive dynamics $T_R$.
- In brute-force enumeration, there is no compression: the system stores every sample rather than forming a compressed representation.

The Learning Definition in T6 (§4.7) explicitly requires that $\Delta L$ be attributable to changes in $R_S$ under internal dynamics $T_R$. Formally:
$$\text{Learning}(S) \iff \frac{\partial L}{\partial R_S} \neq 0 \wedge \dot{R}_S = T_R(R_S)$$
Random search and brute force have $\frac{\partial L}{\partial R_S} = 0$ (the internal structure doesn't change; only the externally selected "best" changes). Natural selection has $\dot{R}_S$ driven by external fitness pressure, not by the organism's own $T_R$.

**Important nuance.** This does not mean that evolution or random search are "unintelligent" in all senses. Evolution is *computationally productive* (it finds solutions), and random search can be *efficient* in certain settings. But EAS distinguishes between *computational success* and *cognitive processing*: a system can succeed without cognition (evolution succeeds through selection, not through understanding), and a system can cognize without immediate success (a learning system may temporarily perform worse as it explores).

**Verdict.** Not a counterexample. Falls outside the Learning condition of T6. These systems may reduce $L$ but do so through external mechanisms, not through the internal quotienting process that EAS identifies with intelligence.

#### Case D: Non-Embedded Systems (The Universe, God's-Eye View)

**Examples.** The universe considered as a whole ($S = E$). A hypothetical omniscient observer. A system that contains its own environment.

**Analysis.** These violate (SC1): embeddedness. If $S = E$, then $\text{card}(R_S)$ need not be less than $\text{card}(E)$, and A1(a) does not apply. Moreover, A1(b) (dynamical non-isomorphism) specifically requires the self-reference structure that arises from $S \subset E$—a system trying to model an environment it is part of. If $S = E$, there is no "outside" to model, and the diagonalization argument does not arise.

This is not a limitation of EAS; it is a *design choice*. EAS is a theory of *embedded cognition*—the cognition of systems that are part of the world they try to understand. The question of what "knowledge" means for a non-embedded system (if such a thing is possible) is a metaphysical question that EAS deliberately does not address.

**Connection to physics.** This parallels the status of reference frames in physics: special relativity applies to inertial frames; general relativity extends to accelerating frames; but the question "what does the universe look like from outside the universe" is meaningless in both frameworks. EAS applies to embedded finite cognitive systems; what "knowledge" means for a non-embedded totality is outside the theory's domain.

**Verdict.** Not a counterexample. Falls outside EAS scope by violation of (SC1).

### 8.3 Summary of Boundary Cases

| Case | Example | Scope condition violated | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1: Static carriers | Books, databases | (SC3) no $T_R$ | Outside scope |
| A2: Frozen cognitive systems | Frozen GPT at inference | None—valid cognitive system | Inside scope: $I_{working}>0$, but not a learning system ($\dot{\theta}=0$) |
| B: Life boundary | Bacteria, immune system | None—all conditions satisfied | Inside scope, low $I(S)$ |
| C: External selection | Random search, evolution | T6 Learning condition: $\frac{\partial L}{\partial R_S} = 0$ | Does not satisfy Learning |
| D: Non-embedded | Universe, omniscient observer | (SC1) $S \not\subsetneq E$ | Outside scope |

### 8.4 Limit Analysis (Degenerate Cases)

A mature mathematical theory is further characterized by its behavior at extreme limits. We analyze four degenerate cases.

#### Limit 1: $d \to 0$ (Vanishing Epistemic Residue)

As the cardinality gap $m - n \to 0$ (system capacity approaching environment complexity), the epistemic residue $d \to 0$.

**Theory predicts:**
- Compression cost $\to 0$ (the system can nearly perfectly represent the environment)
- Concepts become arbitrarily fine-grained (the equivalence classes approach singletons)
- Intelligence $I(S) \to 0$ (there is little left to learn)
- The system approaches an *ideal observer*

**Physical interpretation:** This limit is never achieved in practice (A1 ensures $d > 0$ for all embedded systems), but it provides a useful idealization. It corresponds to Shannon's channel capacity: the theoretical upper bound on information transfer in a noiseless channel.

#### Limit 2: $I(S) \to 0$ (Vanishing Intelligence)

A system with $I(S) = 0$ is at cognitive equilibrium: its representation no longer improves.

**Sub-cases:**
- $I(S) = 0$ with $d > 0$: The system has converged to a stable but imperfect representation (a "local minimum" of cognitive loss). Example: a frozen model, or a mature organism in a stable environment.
- $I(S) = 0$ with $d = 0$: The system has perfectly modeled its environment. Impossible by A1.

**Physical interpretation:** The $I(S) \to 0$ limit corresponds to thermodynamic equilibrium: the system has reached maximum entropy (maximum cognitive compression) and no further work can be extracted.

#### Limit 3: $T_R \to \text{id}$ (Frozen Dynamics)

When the internal dynamics approaches the identity (no internal state change), the system becomes a static mapping.

**Theory predicts:**
- The system is a quotient structure but no longer quotienting (Sub-case A1 above: a purely static carrier)
- It embodies knowledge but does not generate new knowledge
- $I(S) = 0$ regardless of the quality of its representation

**Physical interpretation:** This is the distinction between *software* and *running software*. A frozen $T_R = \text{id}$ is like a compiled program on disk: it contains computational potential but performs no computation.

#### Limit 4: $S \to E$ (Boundary of Embeddedness)

As the system approaches the size of its environment ($n \to m$), A1(a) approaches its boundary.

**Theory predicts:**
- The pigeonhole principle still applies (any non-trivial finite $n < m$ forces non-injectivity)
- But the cardinality gap becomes arbitrarily small
- The dynamical non-isomorphism (A1(b)) becomes the binding constraint: even with $n$ close to $m$, self-reference prevents isomorphism
- The system approaches the limit of "nearly perfect representation, still not isomorphic"

**Physical interpretation:** This limit corresponds to the question: "Can a system almost perfectly model its environment?" EAS answers: yes, it can approach arbitrarily close, but it can never reach perfect isomorphism. The gap is always non-zero, no matter how small. This is the recognition-theoretic analog of the speed of light: approachable but unreachable.

### 8.5 Anticipated Objections

We anticipate the following objections from readers and address them preemptively.

**Objection 1.** *"A frozen LLM clearly exhibits intelligent behavior during inference. If EAS distinguishes between 'cognition within sessions' and 'learning across sessions,' isn't this distinction ad hoc?"*

**Response.** The distinction is not ad hoc; it follows directly from the formal structure. EAS identifies intelligence with the rate of cognitive loss reduction *driven by internal dynamics* (§4.7). During inference, a frozen LLM has genuine internal dynamics at the working memory level (KV cache evolution, attention pattern shifts), so $I_{working}(S) > 0$. The system *is* cognizing. However, these working memory changes are not consolidated into the parameter layer ($\dot{\theta} = 0$), so the system does not *learn* in the persistent sense. This is a structural fact about the architecture, not a definitional convenience designed to exclude LLMs.

The deeper point is that EAS predicts this two-tier structure is *necessary* for any system that performs well on fixed tasks but cannot adapt to novel ones. The parameter layer provides stability (crystallized knowledge); the working memory layer provides flexibility (fluid processing). The inability to transfer working memory changes back to parameters is not a bug—it is a consequence of T5: the two functions serve different adaptive constraints and must be separated to avoid the self-referential bias problem (§4.6).

**Objection 2.** *"AlphaGo during a game has fixed parameters. By your definition, it has $I(S)=0$. But it beats the world champion."*

**Response.** AlphaGo during a game is performing online computation through Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), which dynamically explores the game tree. The MCTS component constitutes a form of online representation change: the search tree is a temporary $R_S$ that evolves during the game. If we model AlphaGo as $S = (\text{policy network} + \text{MCTS tree})$, then $\dot{R}_S \neq 0$ during gameplay (the MCTS tree grows and prunes). So EAS classifies AlphaGo-during-gameplay as having $I(S) > 0$ through the MCTS component, and $I(S) = 0$ for the frozen policy network component alone. The distinction between the fixed network and the dynamic search tree is precisely the distinction between crystallized and fluid intelligence.

**Objection 3.** *"GANs have only two modules (Generator and Discriminator). T5 says $\chi_{\min}=3$. So GANs are counterexamples."*

**Response.** First, GANs are notoriously unstable and suffer from mode collapse—this is precisely what T5a predicts for systems whose representations do not support a functionally decodable verification stream ($I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) > 0$). Second, in practice, successful GAN training requires extensive external engineering (learning rate scheduling, gradient penalty, spectral normalization) that functions as an *external Verifier stream*. The training infrastructure—not the GAN architecture itself—provides the missing third decodable stream. This is why GANs are harder to train than Actor-Critic or Predictive Coding systems that have intrinsic functional trisection.

**Objection 4.** *"The Scope Theorem makes EAS unfalsifiable—any counterexample can be dismissed as 'outside the scope.'"*

**Response.** The scope conditions (SC1)–(SC4) are independently motivated by physical and logical considerations, not by ad hoc exclusion of counterexamples. (SC1) is the physical fact of embedding. (SC2) is the physical fact of finite resources. (SC3) is the definition of a dynamical system. (SC4) is the definition of an active (non-frozen) system. These are not arbitrary restrictions; they define what a "cognitive system" *is*. Moreover, EAS remains falsifiable within its scope: T5 (no two-module open-ended system) is directly testable; T7 (scaling bound $I \leq m^2$) is quantitatively testable; the Learning Definition excludes specific mechanisms. The Scope Theorem delineates the domain; it does not immunize the theory within that domain.

### 8.6 Falsifiability Profile

A theory that explains every possible observation explains nothing. To guard against *immunization by reinterpretation*—the charge that any cognitive phenomenon can be retroactively accommodated by EAS—we state explicit falsifiability conditions. Each condition identifies a specific empirical or mathematical finding whose discovery would require revision or abandonment of the corresponding EAS claim.

**Falsifiability Condition F1 (against T5a).** *If a system is demonstrated to exhibit stable, persistent open-ended adaptation in a non-stationary environment while its functional dimension satisfies $\chi < 3$ (i.e., fewer than three functionally decodable semantic streams can be independently recovered by any admissible decoding basis), then Theorem T5a is falsified.*

Operationalization: Construct an architecture with $\chi = 2$ (e.g., a two-stream system where $Z_V$ is fully reconstructible from $Z_G$ and $Z_E$) and test whether it can maintain open-ended learning without catastrophic degradation. If it succeeds, the decodability hypothesis fails. Linear probe experiments on existing successful continual learning systems can also test this: if no system with $\chi \geq 3$ is required for stability, T5a is weakened.

**Falsifiability Condition F2 (against A1).** *If the epistemic residue $d$ can be driven to zero (or made arbitrarily small) in a finite embedded cognitive system—such that the system achieves near-isomorphic representation of its environment—yet the system still exhibits bounded cognition or systematic failures, then Axiom A1 is incomplete and the residue-based account of cognitive limitation is insufficient.*

Operationalization: If a finite system is constructed with $n$ sufficiently close to $m$ that $d \approx 0$, and it still fails on tasks that should be trivially solvable given its representational capacity, then the source of failure lies elsewhere (e.g., computational complexity, optimization dynamics) and A1 alone does not explain cognitive bounds.

**Falsifiability Condition F3 (against T4.2 / Self-Validation Impossibility).** *If a finite cognitive system is demonstrated in which the Verifier stream carries no information beyond what is reconstructible from the Generator and Executor streams ($I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) = 0$ to within statistical precision), yet the system remains stable under persistent open-ended adaptation—exhibiting no reward hacking, wireheading, specification gaming, or self-referential degradation—then the Self-Validation Impossibility theorem is empirically falsified.*

Operationalization: Train a system where $Z_V$ is architecturally forced to be a deterministic function of $Z_G$ and $Z_E$, and test whether it exhibits pathological self-referential behavior (reward hacking, mode collapse, loss of calibration) at rates significantly above a control system with independent $Z_V$. If no significant difference is observed, the irreducibility of the verification stream is not empirically necessary.

**Falsifiability Condition F4 (against T7).** *If cognitive capacity is empirically demonstrated to scale super-quadratically with system size—i.e., $I(S) > C \cdot m^2$ for some constant $C$ and arbitrarily large $m$—then the capacity bound T7 is falsified.*

Operationalization: Measure scaling behavior across cognitive systems of increasing size. If performance grows faster than $O(m^2)$ consistently across multiple architectures and domains, T7 requires revision.

**Summary of the falsifiability structure.** The table below organizes the four conditions by the EAS claim they target and the empirical signature that would refute it:

| Condition | Targets | Falsifying observation |
|---|---|---|
| F1 | T5a (Functional Decodability) | Stable open-ended system with $\chi < 3$ |
| F2 | A1 (Finite Embedding) | $d \to 0$ yet bounded cognition persists |
| F3 | T4.2 (Self-Validation Impossibility) | $I(Z_V; W \mid Z_G, Z_E) = 0$ with no pathology |
| F4 | T7 (Capacity Bound) | $I(S)$ scaling super-quadratically in $m$ |

These conditions collectively ensure that EAS is not a *universal re-description system* that absorbs all observations post hoc. Each core claim carries specific, in-principle-testable risk of refutation. The theory is not unfalsifiable; it is *structurally risky*—every major theorem names the finding that would defeat it.

## 9. Conclusion

We have presented the Epistemic Axiomatic System (EAS), a minimal axiomatic foundation for epistemology based on a single axiom: finite cognitive systems cannot be isomorphic to their environments. From this axiom, eight theorems follow, establishing the necessity of distinction, compression, concepts, laws, truth, functional decodability and dynamical trisection, intelligence, and cognitive capacity bounds. The entire chain has been mechanically verified in Lean 4.

EAS does not claim to be a complete theory of cognition. It claims to be a *foundation*: a single principle from which the necessary structure of cognition can be derived. Whether this foundation corresponds to reality is an empirical question—one that we have begun to address through concrete predictions (P1–P3) but have not yet resolved.

The central message of EAS can be stated in one sentence:

> **Finite cognition is necessarily quotient cognition.**

A cognitive system does not—and *cannot*—copy its environment. It *quotients* its environment: partitioning the world into equivalence classes through the map induced by non-injective representation. This is not a limitation to be overcome; it is the defining structure of what cognition *is*. From this single insight, the entire architecture of cognition—concepts, laws, truth, intelligence, and the necessity of functional trisection ($\chi \geq 3$)—follows as mathematical consequence.

The deepest structural insight is that A1 *recurs* at the meta-cognitive level. Just as a finite system cannot preserve all environmental distinctions (object level), it cannot simultaneously maintain three independent meta-distinctions—long-term structure, online execution, and error attribution—within a single undivided representation. Theorem T5a (Functional Decodability) establishes that three *functionally decodable* semantic streams must exist—not as physically separate modules or orthogonal subspaces, but as independently recoverable information channels compatible with distributed representations, superposition, and sparse coding. Theorem T4.2 (Self-Validation Impossibility) further shows that this trisection provides a common structural lens for understanding Gödel incompleteness, Turing undecidability, Goodhart's law, and reward hacking: no finite system can validate its own representations. This recursive application of A1 transforms functional trisection from a contingent design pattern into an inevitable structural consequence: T5a is A1 applied to the system's own cognitive space.

This insight, grounded in a single axiom and verified by machine, is our contribution.
